In her Majesty’s service

Sweat ran freely down the red-haired Englishman’s temples, smearing the jungle camo on his clean-shaven face. A knife blade covered in dry mud sat in his left hand, while his other hand made a rough circle in the air. It was a hand signal for the command to encircle the target.
Five men melted away into the green humid jungle in a heartbeat, as if they had been an optical illusion. They moved fast; faster than what Ethan had believed them capable of. He decided to stand back for a little while more, and see how they could handle their approach on their own.
All of them stopped dead in their tracks and hunched low when they heard noise, probably from the direction of the clearing up ahead. The two men that formed the edge of the squad aimed their rifles outwards from the squad, as if a switch had been thrown. The point man laid himself flat with the silken grace of a cat, knife drawn in his right hand while his left hand reached out for his Colt zero-point-forty-five. The rearguard moved like a pair of dancers, covering each other’s back, both aiming their rifles from shoulder-high, safeties clicked off to single-shot selection.
In that cautious small diamond formation they moved as a whole, each step forward made with practiced ease and well-earned confidence, the mark of a well-trained soldier. For all intends and purposes, and as far as Ethan was concerned, he had done a good job training them. What did bother him though was that they had yet to fire a shot in anger. He knew that nothing tests a man like real combat.
Though they moved precisely as they had been taught, and seemed quick and stealthy enough, Ethan couldn’t really tell if they just went through the motions or if they were really up for the challenge. After all, no matter how much one practices before a show, it’s the opening night that makes or breaks it.
Ethan’s free hand was wrapped tightly around a metal L-shaped trigger attached to a small box no larger than a pack of cigarettes, from which a run of cables protruded and got lost somewhere under the rich jungle tapestrie of green and brown. With a tight grin, Ethan whispered to himself with the expectation of a job well done:
“Showtime, lads.”
When he double-clicked the trigger the first thing that attracted everyone’s attention were the flashes. And then came the shockwaves, their force pummeling at everyone, making every ribcage vibrate intensely as if tremors had seized them. And that was just the opening note.
Small gun fire erupted from various well-placed locations, the muzzle flashes dotting the lavish scenery as if hundreds of photographers clicked in rapid succession.
The scouts instantly dived for cover, falling prone while keeping their arcs of fire fimly fixed to their own particular zone of responsibility. A wry smile formed on Ethan’s face: he seemed to be enjoying the performance.
The five trainees were on their bellies, rolling and crawling over the jungle bed in an effort to find as much cover as possible in mere moments. They exchanged quick glances and made repetitive hand motions to each other, communicating silently trying to establish a point where they could fall back away from the guns blazing at them.
Every tree trunk and low bush or gully counted for gold at times like these, and the five Marine scouts from the 3rd Marine division made excellent use of their surroundings. Very much so that Ethan felt a twang of pride at seeing his handiwork in action. They were his men, and they seemed every bit as professional as he intended them to be.
They quickly abandoned their exposed position for a slightly shallow depression with good cover, fat tree trunks strewn around them with thick bush for cover and a clear view on all sides. A defendable position from which they could easily spot a move against them and fall back while they harassed their enemy firing and moving in turns. Ethan’s mind flashed with the sudden realisation they were good enough to be Royal Marine scouts, not just Nigerian troops.
He almost felt a hint of regret he couldn’t be there with them when it really mattered, when the guns pointing towards them were not loaded with blanks; when the explosive charges half a mile away were real shells and mortars falling right on them.
Almost, as Ethan reminded himself inside his head, was the eminent keyword: This was not his war, not by a far chance, and setting up mock engagements such as this one was as close as he wanted to get to another warzone. Except for the times when he itched, except for the times he woke up in the middle of the night drenched in sweat, knowing it wasn’t the tropical heat that left him sleepless thereafter, but faces all too well remembered and defined.
The team of five men had dug themselves in taking solid cover. The gunfire died down soon afterwards, and Ethan believed there was no point in continuing tracking and crossing the jungle. He had seen what he wanted to see, and that was a team of scouts, not a bunch of gung-ho Nigerians hollering with rifles raised firing on auto, vying for Igbo blood.
Ethan raised himself above the brushes and bellowed as if he wanted the cadets back at Sandhurst to hear him clear as rain:
“End of session! Fine job!”
The team of five gave no sign of sight or sound that they had heard their instructor. Ethan repeated himself with less enthusiasm:
“Come on, games’ over! Let’s head back for some rest, off we go.”
Still, nothing could be now heard other than the usual sounds of the jungle, the distant cries of birds and monkeys, and the constant buzzing of mosquitoes and other assorted exotic insects.
Ethan furrowed his brow and briefly considered that the team might have actually extracted themselves without him noticing, and decided to check the small depression himself. With a hurried pace, he made his way through the brush and in a few moments reached the position he had last seen them take cover in.
He saw noone, but suddenly felt a piece of metal poking him through his left side, right between his ribs, poised to pierce his heart at a single thrust. He had been caught unawares, and once he looked sideways to his assailant, he saw Onko, the team scout leader smiling wildly, his bright white teeth a stark contrast to his camouflaged dark face. A little stunned and a little bit surprised, Ethan barely had time to let off a curse:
“Bugger me, you’ve grown into a real scout Onko.”
To which the nigerian marine responded with a heavy accent, no longer wearing the same disarming smile:
“You grow soft, Captain Whittmore.”
Onko then sheathed the knife once more, and the four other men suddenly appeared from quite unlikely and widespread places, from any one of which they could have put a bullet through Ethan at their leisure.
Ethan nodded and made a small sigh, as if acknowledging he had indeed let his guard down and been overcome, exactly what he tought his recruits should never happen, not even while not in the battlefield. Cultivating a little paranoia went a long way in keeping a man alive, especially in a civil war where telling a friend from an enemy was not as clean a business as shooting at one.
Ethan did manage to save some face though when he pointed at his feet to a small bump in the ground, leaves cluttered all over it and said:
“See now Dbele, that’s a mark for a landmine. You might have killed at least one man when you fell back here.”
Onko was far from smiling at that information, and with a mixed expression somewhere between anger and disappointment he said to Ethan, making it sound almost like an accusation:
“There was no place else to fall back and cover, Captain. It was a death trap then, for sure. What could we have done different? That’s not fair, sir.”
The word ‘sir’ had a strange ring to it, and that probably was because it was meant to sound off-putting. Ethan only cared to answer briefly right before he turned about and started walking to the gathering area of the training field:
“Who said anything about war being fair, sergeant?”
* * *
In the warm light of neon orange, Ethan searched his pockets for the some small change, but to no avail. He rarely needed dimes and pennies when he made his cosmopolitan visits to the center of Lagos, but using the jukebox in Loui’s bar was such an occasion, and even more so a favourite habit.
In fact, Ethan was not a rare visitor to Loui’s establishment, and most would instantly recognise him. He was widely known as the Englishman, both to the few locals and the numerous extraordinary foreigners that frequented the Metropolitaine.
Ethan maintained that he only came for the regularly up-to-date jukebox, whilst his tab indicated a thirsty sort of music lover with a certain taste for fine malts.
Malts such as the unusually fine scotch that Loui kept stashed for the well-off or even well-liked customer. He had simply decided to thank God there was good scotch to be found in a place like Nigeria and even more so in a bar like Loui’s. To make the realisation even more mind-numbing for Ethan, there was good scotch to be found during a civil war; a state of affairs that was mirrored in the Metropolitaine’s crude and shanty decoration, or more appropriately its blatant lack thereof.
A couple of ceiling fans still worked despite what one might expect at first sight, barely keeping the air from going stale.
It was with his usual wide grin that Louis, the proprietor, approached Ethan and offered him a penny before bowing slightly, impeccably dressed in a striped jacket, white pressed shirt, bowtie and smart pants, an air of the thirties Paris about him. Ethan accepted the coin gladly and immediately dropped it in the slot, while Louis chimed in his blatantly french but not unbearable accent:
“Compliments of the house, Capitain. The usual? Little ice, double fine scotch?”
Ethan smiled wryly and nodded while selecting a song in the jukebox, his index finger searching for the correct button to press. He pushed ‘Under my thumb’, and turned to reply to Louis before setting off to settle in his usual barstool:
“Very fine scotch.”
“Nothing but Scotland’s finest, Mr. Whittmore.”
Ethan sat on the barstool, his eyes staring at the glasses and various bottles of liquor neatly arrayed and featuring prominently in the shelves behind the bar. When he next spoke, it was with a feeling of relief:
“I’d never thought I’d say this, but God bless Nigeria.”
Louis had assumed his proper place behind the bar, picked up a bottle of one of his finest malts from a cupboard below along with a short glass, and said smiling wryly while he poured:
“I’m from Guiana though.”
“Ah, and Scotch is from Scotland but it knows no borders. Come on then, pour one yourself.”
The unusually tall, lank bartender complied and picked up a shot glass which he filled promptly and raised to a toast:
“To all the thirsty men.”
The Rolling Stones song had started playing in the background. Ethan raised his own glass of scotch and made a toast as well:
“To Mick Jagger and his crew.”
They both gulped down their drinks in one go. Ethan made a slight motion with one hand, indicating the cupboard below.
Louis went through the motions of pouring another glass of scotch and asked his regular customer for the past year or so with his usual air of cool affection:
“How’s life treating you?”
Ethan’s tone was lighthearted, almost flippant when he said:
“A little better than horseshit. I’m not complaining though, I’m still here, aren’t I?”
Louis laughed politely and nodded before replying:
“I’d be out of a job if you weren’t. Woe be me if something should happen to you.”
“Just keep the scotch coming and I’ll manage.”
Loui let the glass of scotch slide across the wooden bar and looked him straight in the eye, his face almost a pale shade of dark under the dim candlelight of the bar:
“So, anything new from your friends in high places?”
Ethan shook his head and brought the glass to his lips, sniffing the aromas:
“No, nothing yet. It’s not easy, you know that. Not to mention the money involved. But it’ll take time Louis. Biafra is considered a little bit more important than your visa.”
Louis shook his head in disappointment, and started polishing a basket full of washed glasses. His eyes were fixed to the task at hand when he replied to Ethan with a mixed feeling of sadness and slight aggravation, his movements lacking his usual crispness and finesse:
“You said by the end of the month, Englishman. Said you knew the ‘ins and outs’, didn’t you? Who will keep Insami and Wadu off my back, I wonder. You, or your crown?”
Ethan kept wearing the same smile that had won him arguments on innumerable occasions, while he kept tapping his fingers to the rhythm of the song playing from the jukebox. After a short uncomfortable silence, he said to Louis:
“It must feel like a kick in the nuts, Louis, but it’s not as bad as you make it sound. Take a look around you. There’s bigger trouble than those two thugs. If they do pop up and act like a couple of tough govs, I’ll make sure they get one in the sack and lose a couple of teeth each. So take that off your mind. What do you have for me this time?”
Louis seemed to relax a bit, his tense mouth loosening into a tight smile. He reached for a tall cupboard with a lock on the handles, and used a small key that hung from a chain around his neck. He opened the cupboard with small, graceful movements of his hands, as if opening an icon or a shrine. In it, a dozen bottles of Cullough sat, dusty and squat, seemingly quite authentic seal, cork and all. At the sight of the bottles of scotch and the prospect of savouring them at his leisure, Ethan’s face lit up and his blue eyes seemed for a moment to sparkle. His voice couldn’t contain his enthusiasm:
“Cullough? Bugger me, I can’t even get close to this stuff even back home. You know the Cullough family is from WW_BIRTHPLACE. That’s William Wallace’s birthplace. Scotch indeed.”
Loui asked with real curiosity while bringing down a bottle of Cullough carefully:
“Never heard of a scotch by that name.”
Ethan blinked twice and was taken slightly aback when the name of the greatest Scotsman that ever lived was unheard of in this part of the world. He nevertheless straightened his back as if he was addressing the lords and ladies of the Backingham palace when he tried to explain to Loui. His accent suddenly became a bit heavier, rolling the l’s:
“William Wallace was the greatest scotsman that ever lived. Fought the English for the freedom of Scotland for over a dozen years. They killed his wife and family and in the end he was betrayed. He gave his all, William Wallace. Biggest set of stones ever.”
Loui looked at Ethan in puzzlement as he uncorked the fine scotch, its aromas of cedar and mead wafted upwards. His question seemed to flatten Ethan’s face right at the moment his nostrils had become so excited:
“But Scotland isn’t free. You serve the Queen of England.”
“That’s not entirely true, I serve the Queen of the United Kingdom.”
“But there’s no Queen of the Scots, is there?”
“She’s also Queen of the Scots. And the Welsh. And the Irish. Well, at least some of the Irish.”
“See, that’s not unlike the situation here in Nigeria. The Igbo are like the Scots, they want to be free. Shouldn’t they be free?”
“I don’t have a say in that. It’s not my job, and it’s not my people. If they can, they will.”
“And I’m just a bartender, Captain. I’m only saying. The Vietnamese, the Angolans, the Igbo, the Scots.”
“The Beatles. The Stones. The Yardbirds. Some of those, I know and like. The rest, maybe I’ve heard of them, but their’s not my kind of tune. Now tend to your bar, please.”
Loui smiled wryly and was about to retort when the door bell rang and attracted his attention. He looked up and pointed with his long bony index finger to a sturdy, tall and fit black man dressed in fatigues, the beret of the Nigerian Marine Corps smartly adorning his head. The man’s eyes peered vehemently through the haze and fog of the smoke and dust that seemed to always twirl lazily in the Metropolitaine.
The soldier’s gaze quickly settled on Ethan, who was spinning around on his barstool to look at the newcomer directly. He cracked a smile and gave a mocking half-salute to the burly man who - judging by his epaulets - appeared to be a brevet Major. The man did not seem to share the same good humor and did not salute, neither did he seem to enjoy smiles and levity a lot. As he approached the bar, Ethan cheerfully made a gesture at the still open cupboard full of Cullough and greeted the man with a proposition:
“James, this is a once in a lifetime chance for a once in a lifetime experience. It’s Cullough. It’s the nectar of the Gods. It’ll be monumental James, getting plastered with the finest scotch in the whole country, with little doubt. So, what is the unhappy occassion of your visit here in uniform? Tell me all about it so I can forget it with the help of Louis and Cullough. Is it remotely serious? Are the Biafrans hurtling shells at Lagos? Can I go home now?”
The heavily set man, who otherwise seemed intimidating and capable of severely wounding a man armed with nothing but his hands, had a strangely calm and serene look in his face, as if he had suddenly drifted far away in his mind to a better place when he said calmly:
“It’s your brother. We have reports their caravan was probably attacked today. Somewhere in the jungle near the border. They never reached <TOWN>.”
Ethan looked suddenly somber and withdrawn. The news were a mood killer to say the least. He looked at James with wary, stern eyes:
“Well, what is the Army going to do about it? The Red Cross is supposed to have its support. Where’s the support, James?”
James took Ethan by the arm and told him in a friendly, casual manner that his external appearance belied:
“Let’s have a drink, Ethan. Let’s talk.”
* * *
Business in the Metropolitaine was in full swing. A small gang of sailors were celebrating one of their mates birthday, following the customary rule of drinking till the botswain comes looking for them. Loui kept a wary eye on a couple of strange figures, but other than that the orders kept coming in and that made him a happy man.
Ethan on the other hand looked far from jovial. Indeed, news of losing contact with his brother had suddenly turned this war into a personal matter, something that every professional soldier tried to avoid. Making warfare personal also meant someone would do something stupid, and stupidity was a well-known but sadly overlooked factor in dying.
James had fallen silent for a couple minutes, silently sipping at his wine, a local unbottled variety without a fancy name or an extravagant price. Ethan was down to his last couple of cigarettes, chain-smoking ever since they sat down to talk. At length, Ethan broke the uncomfortable silence:
“I should have pulled some strings when he told me he was going in with the Red Cross. Red tape, paperwork, passport trouble. Surely someone you know in the Interior could have been of some help. Maybe forced him to stay in Britain.”
James motioned a definite ‘no’ with his head, eyes closed shut. He then had another sip before answering:
“You know there are many ways to come into Nigeria. If your brother wanted to come, he would have found his own. There’s nothing you could have really done to prevent him from coming here in the first place.”
Ethan drew heavily on his cigarette and exhaled briskly. He spoke with some irritation:
“True enough, that. Maybe you could have detained him when their caravan set off for Biafra? I could have spoken some more sense into him.”
James was as calm as before, answering with a flat and emotionless voice, trying to calm down Ethan as well:
“Not with a Red Cross caravan. They’d rage about the Geneva convention, make too much fuss. Nigeria’s image is not exactly pristine, and noone likes headaches from the press. No, we could not have told your brother to just stay put. It was your job to do so if you wanted. Not mine, and certainly not Nigeria’s.”
Ethan put out his cigarette, drank the rest of his drink in one go and motioned his empty glass to Loui who seemed to notice almost immediately. Ethan then looked straight into James’ eyes, a set of dark eyes accented by the small bit of white that surrounded them. He tried to calm himself and find the appropriate words:
“You are right about that. It was my job. I should have just whipped him good like when we were still ten years old. But he’s a grown man, a doctor. He has his duties, his obligations. Like I have mine. Though I still think it’s stupid, at least he acted like the man he’s supposed to be. He wanted to help, he signed up with the Red Cross. Never saw how lying down on the grass all day long, doing weed and fucking like rabbits could stop people from dying. ”
There was a pause. Loui was returning to their table with Ethan’s refill of Cullough, and a clean ashtray. Ethan took his time before continuing, and nodded his thanks to Loui who bowed slightly and fleeted off to serve some other corner in the Metropolitaine. James had rested his arms on the table, his frame and build too large to comfortably seat himself in the Metropolitaine’s simple chairs. Ethan took a mouthful of Cullough and flinched when he felt it burn down through his throat and into his stomach. He then went on:
”I know war’s no place for idealists and romantics, that’s probably why I’m still alive. I’ve seen it real up close and personal and I’d like none of that come knocking at my door again. Maybe I even think Andy’s doing a better job than I ever could. I mean, in the grander scheme of things, him being a doctor and all that. Can’t really tell why I didn’t stop him. I just couldn’t, you know?”
James blinked languidly while sipping the last bit of his wine before replying:
“Someone has to try and save the world. People like your brother think they can.”
Ethan’s face grimaced his slight annoyance at the suggestion, and lit up one of the last cigarettes in his pack.
“Well, I don’t know. I’m not exactly in the business of saving people, am I? You could say we’re sort of antagonists, me and Andy. At least, we used to be in opposite teams when we balled.”
“I didn’t know you play cricket.”
“Haven’t ever since I got a leg injury in Kenya. Nasty business that was. Almost got meself killed. Young, stupid and rash. Also, quite a lucky bastard.”
James expression seemed to change somewhat. He removed his hands from the table and for a moment sat still, looking at Ethan intensely. He then ordered another drink from Loui, who seemingly kept a watchful eye at their table more so than other tables and nodded promptly, going at the back for a couple minutes. When James spoke next, he was lighting up Ethan’s last cigarette, receiving nothing but the stunned surprise and deep furrow of Ethan:
“Were you any good at it?”
“What, cricket? I thought you didn’t smoke.”
“I’m a man of many talents. And some vices as well.”
“Well, I hope you’re not a Rolling Stones fan as well. It’d be a real crime to find out you’ve been hiding that too.”
James drew on his cigarette and threw his head back to blow off some smoke. He was smiling when he pointed to Ethan and said:
“Not much to hide, Ethan. Sometimes I smoke. Usually when I’m alone. Which is really very little time. Barely enough for a smoke.”
Ethan nodded with a sly grin on his face. He sipped another mouthful from his glass of scotch which was disappearing fast. James went on:
“I thought you were more like the football sort. Maybe even rugby. Or is golf the real scotsman’s sport?”
Ethan laughed despite his awry mood and laid back on the small chair:
“Sometimes I wish they’d made drinking an official sport. I’m sure we’d have a good chance at the World Championship.”
James was toying with the ice in his glass when a slight grin formed on his face, droplets of sweat running down his forehead, glistening dimly in the hazy, poor lighting of the Metropolitaine:
“A better chance than cricket, true enough. A bad leg won’t leave you behind.”
“It isn’t just the bad leg. I’m not a cricket fan really, but Andy loves it. At least as a kid he did. Used to drag me along. The bad leg is just a reminder.”
James seemed to stiffen suddenly. He straightened his back before reaching for his glass, but his words had a hint of mirth in them:
“Memoris can hurt as well. What happened in Kenya? Tell me, I’m a fool for romantic stories.”
Ethan smiled while trying to down the last mouthful of Cullogh from his glass, and pointed to his mouth. When he did swallow it, his face contorted from the intense feeling of a wet fireball going down to his belly, but managed to respond in a timely manner:
“Kenya, right. Nineteen sixty three. The yankees had just invaded Vietnam and my team had shipped out to Kenya as part of a counter-insurgency operation. The plan was to turn the various guerilla parties on each other, feeding disinformation and using scare tactics. We were supposed to make contact with a few loyalist bands, and organize a small force that would stop the insurrection in its heart. It was pretty complex, far-fetched and too optimistic. Very few men. No small wonder it failed. Of course that’s not why we lost Kenya.”
Ethan stopped for a moment when Loui came with James’ new glass of wine. He picked up Ethan’s empty glass and nodded with a wink, before leaving them alone once again. Somewhere in the opposite corner of the Metropolitaine a loud crashing noise was heard and Ethans fleeting gaze revealed the bunch of sailors strewn around over lots of broken glasses and a couple of upturned tables. A loud chorus of laughters was heard and once the noise resumed its regularly high levels, Ethan decided to go on:
“We were trying to rendezvous with a loyalist cell in the <KENYA_REGION1>, scout out the area, evaluate targets and then organize a strike to disorganize and demoralise the local rebel cells. Guerilla warfare textbook case. Anyway, first couple of nights in the jungle, all fine and dandy; nothing but mosquitoes, fucking jungle weather and bad sleep, which was to be expected in the bloody jungle.
On the third night though, I woke up not with a kick in the me bollocks. I realise my hands are all tied up. Can’t fucking believe what’s going on; whole squad captured like a bunch of campers. The sentries were talking about the last fucking Millwall-Chelsea game. Long story short, we got caught with our pants down. They didn’t kill us outright, that would have been stupid and of no use. The Kenyans needed leverage, public approval, anything that would convince the parliament to say ‘balls off with Kenya, it’s not worth it’.
We were eleven men on that squad. At first I thought they would exchange us, but then we noticed we were going around in circles somewhere in <KENYA_REGION2>, nothing like a plan. Like a long range patrol or whatnot. Our heavy gunner, McKinley, tried to make a run for it one day. He stumbled and tripped, dropped twenty feet down a cliff and broke both his legs. Their leader, a big brute of a man, shot him in the back of the head. Even said he was being merciful. That was when fucking bells started ringing in our heads.
That cunt had it coming for him. We decided to have a go at them all together. We stopped for fresh water by a small river one day. While they were bathing, we ganged up on two of our guards. That’s when I got stabbed in the knee and tore up the ligaments. Nobody died though. Except that man. I drowned that fucker. Held him down in the river till my hands turned blue and I couldn’t feel them. First time I killed a man with my bare hands. We then hiked back through the jungle, the Kenyan’s in tow. Debriefing them gave some insight into the area, but a fat lot of good that did in the end. I got a demerit for losing McKinley and getting captured. Should’ve been a major by now.”
Ethan paused and drained his glass, real thirst driving him. James was looking at Ethan through bloodshot eyes, his glass of wine empty once more. He sat upright in his chair, drew audibly through his large nostrils on the thick air of the Metropolitaine and made a hand signal for another round of drinks, making sure that Louis brought two glasses of Cullough. Ethan sat across him, his gaze fixed on the ceiling fan above them. He looked distantly thoughtful, even slightly grim. He turned his eyes to his empty glass and spoke with a touch of anger behind each sentence:
“I need to find my brother. I’ve never left a man behind in my life. Brought everyone back.”
James seemed at once somber and surprised, his eyes narrowing dangerously:
“Ethan, there’s a war going on. Going out deep in the jungle alone is.. A fool’s errand.”
Ethan picked up their drinks from Louis’ passing tray in mid-air, and replied:
“It’s not an errand, it’s something I must do. And I’m no fool either, I’m a scot.”
James stare had begun to pierce through Ethan’s eyes, casting a gaze hard as stone upon him:
“You must be out of your mind.”
Ethan nodded in agreement, and James voice had a slow quality about it that showed his determination:
“You’ll need all the help you can get then. If it’s going to have any meaning at all, that is.”
Ethan cracked a smile and drank a tiny sip of Cullough, noticing he was almost out of scotch. Louis then appeared out of nowhere with the grace of a dancer. He offered them the bottle of Cullough he had opened earlier. There was barely enough scotch in it for just another drink.
“Gentlemen, compliments of the house. And you can keep the bottle too, if you like.”
Ethan nodded his thanks and laughed despite himself, while James still sat there looking at Ethan seemingly unable to discern whether or not the man was simply drunk and already grieving, making up ideas. He asked Ethan, the stress in his voice showing he wanted to be convinced:
“You are sure you are going to do this? I want to help. But I want to know I’m not risking my neck for some jungle antiques, Ethan Whittmore. And it could mean my neck. I need you to be deadly serious. All the way.”
Ethan’s reply was as sharp as his pervasive eyes:
“I got nothing left other than my brother. Nothing that matters. My job’s a joke nowdays. Can’t settle down somewhere. No woman can stand around me longer than my cock in her cunt. Andy’s all I got left that hasn’t hurt me. What good am I for if don’t go looking for him?”
James shrugged, his large set of shoulders tensing up his fatigues almost to the point of tearing. He then told Ethan:
“He could be already dead, you know that. Sure he might be a white English doctor, but no matter how useful he may prove, bullets are not very picky.”
Ethan was not backing out:
“Then I’ll bring back the body to Inverness and lay him in the ground. I’ll do what I can, James. Anything.”
“So, are you going to just quit tomorrow?”
Ethan smiled bitterly and said:
“Maybe I should. They wouldn’t let me though. Operational needs, lack of personnel, that sort of thing. The service wants to fuck you three ways to Australia if they can. Can’t even put in for leave, not at such a short notice. Can you arrange some sort of training exercise? Any reason that will demand me being attached to somewhere outside Lagos.
